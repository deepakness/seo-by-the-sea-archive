## Google Ranking Signals

Learning about SEO helps to understand what search engineers might consider when deciding how pages rank in response to search queries. So I’ve been trying to learn about Google ranking signals by reading and writing about patents from the search engine. I’ve blogged about patents from Google, Yahoo, Microsoft, and Apple since 2005 while exploring what those might say are ranking signals for over a decade.

Spokespeople from Google have said, “Just because we have a patent on something, doesn’t mean we are using it.” The first time I heard that from them was after Go Daddy began advertising domain registrations of up to 10 years after a Google patent ([Information Retrieval Based on Historical Data](https://www.seobythesea.com/2011/10/revisiting-googles-information-retrieval-based-upon-historical-data/)) said that Google might look at the length of domain registration as a ranking signal. This was based on the thought that a “spammer would likely only register a domain for one year.” (Actually, many people register domains for one year, and have their registrations on auto-renewal, so a one-year registration is not evidenced that a person registering a domain for just one year is a spammer.)

Trying to identify whether something is a Google Ranking Signal can mean more than reading patents from Google. It also means reading blog posts from Google and interviews from their webmaster evangelists. It also means testing what patents say, to see if something that might be a Google Ranking Signal affects the rankings of pages.

Sometimes Google does not put in place processes that they might describe in a patent. If I write about one here, it is often because it is recently released. They are worth testing to see if Google has implemented a Google Ranking Signal that may be described in a patent. I also like that patents tell about problems they perceive on the web, and those patents intend to solve those problems. So looking at patents to see the approaches used by the search engine to solve problems can be worth doing.

I’ve included Google ranking signals that are a little older, but most of the things in this post are from the past five years. They often point to blog posts about Google rankings and the patents that go with them. This list is a compilation of blog posts that I have been working on for years. It is based on:

- Hours of searching through patent filings
- Reading blog posts from within the Search and SEO industries
- Reading through many patents that I didn’t write about
- Reading many patents that I have written about.

If you have questions about any of the Google ranking signals I’ve listed, please ask about them in the comments.

Some of the patents I have blogged about have not been implemented by Google yet but could be. Google files patents to protect the intellectual property behind their ideas and the work that their search engineers and testing teams put into those ideas. It is worth looking at, reading, and understanding many of these patents because they provide insights into ideas that Google may have explored when developing Google rankings. They may give you ideas of things that you may want to explore and questions to keep in mind when optimizing a site. In addition, patents inspire people to innovate and invent and understand new ideas and inventions.

I have added a section to this post covering Additional Google Ranking Signals after realizing that I missed at least one that I thought was worth including and anticipating adding more to this page. I may have to create a part 2 to this post at some point in time. I will decide when it seems to make sense.


## Organic Search Google Ranking Signals

Organic Google Ranking Signals can impact and influence the order of pages in Web-based search results. Those are results that aren’t narrowed down into more specific niches such as News or Local. News Results and Local Results are influenced by other ranking signals than other organic results. Understanding the differences can be helpful when you try to increase the visibility of sites in search results.

It isn’t unusual to see Organic search results blended with non-organic search results because of Google’s approach to [Universal search](https://www.seobythesea.com/2008/06/how-google-universal-search-and-blended-results-may-work/) and their use of a [one box](https://searchengineland.com/googles-onebox-patent-application-10325) at the top of search results. This means that there may be many different ranking signal types used for any one query.

Some sites focus on providing informational results and transactional results for a query, and result from that blend both together.

**1. Domain Age and Rate of Linking**

Google has a patent called [Document scoring based on document inception date](https://patents.google.com/patent/US8521749B2/en). It tells us they will often use the date that they first crawl a site or the first time they see a document referenced in another site as the site’s age. The patent also says Google may look at the links pointing to a site. It will then calculate what the average rate of links pointed to a site may be to use to rank a site based upon that linking.

**Action items**: Create evergreen content that people will find value in over time and continue to link to. As people continue to link to it, Google will continue to see value in that content.

**2. Use of Keywords**

Matt Cutts wrote a newsletter for librarians where he explained how Google crawled the web and made an inverted index of the Web with terms found on Documents from the pages to match up with query terms when people searched. That shows us the importance of Keywords in queries and that Google finds pages that contain those keywords as an important part of performing searches. That newsletter is here: [https://www.analistaseo.es/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/How-Google-Index-Rank.pdf](https://www.analistaseo.es/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/How-Google-Index-Rank.pdf)

**Action items**: Perform keyword research for every page of a site. Make sure that you use the keywords you have mapped out to specific pages upon those pages, and have used the keywords in important parts of the pages, such as:

- Page titles
- Main headings
- Anchor text pointed to the pages
- Body content on the page

There is no need to have a specific percentage of usage of those words on the page, but you need to include the keywords you have selected for a page to rank for that term or phrase.

Perform audience and keyword research, and use keywords that your targeted audience will use to search for your pages and expect to see those pages.

**3. Related Phrases**

Google Ranking Signals can change over time. Google recently updated its phrase-based indexing patent, which now tells us pages with more related phrases on them can rank higher than pages with fewer related phrases. That patent used to say that the number of related phrases on a page could lead to a page being boosted in search results or demoted. That patent is: [Phrase-based searching in an information retrieval system](http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO1&Sect2=HITOFF&d=PALL&p=1&u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsrchnum.htm&r=1&f=G&l=50&s1=9,990,421.PN.&OS=PN/9,990,421&RS=PN/9,990,421). Related phrases are phrases that are complete phrases that may predict the topic a page appears upon. Google might look at the queries that a page targets. The highest-ranking pages for those query terms may be used to see which meaningful complete phrases frequently occur (or co-occur) on those high-ranking pages.

I wrote about an update of this patent in [Google Phrase-Based Indexing Updated](https://gofishdigital.com/phrase-based-indexing-updated/). Google tells us about how they are indexing related phrases in an inverted index (like the term-based inverted index from #2) in the patent [Index server architecture using tiered and sharded phrase posting lists](https://patents.google.com/patent/US7693813B1)

**Action Items**: Look up the query terms you have mapped out to pages on your site and identify the most commonly occurring phrases on those pages. Consider adding some of those to your pages to capture semantic themes that happen on those pages. You can use some of those phrases as anchor text where appropriate, impacting acting as expert links from your pages. Don’t imitate the pages you find related words upon, but be inspired by them.

**4. Keywords in Main Headings, Lists, and Titles**

I wrote the post [Google Defines Semantic Closeness as a Ranking Signal](https://www.seobythesea.com/2010/05/google-defines-semantic-closeness-as-a-ranking-signal/) after reading the patent, [Document ranking based on semantic distance between terms in a document](http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO2&Sect2=HITOFF&u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsearch-adv.htm&r=1&p=1&f=G&l=50&d=PTXT&S1=7,716,216.PN.&OS=pn/7,716,216&RS=PN/7,716,216). The Abstract of this patent tells us that:


> Techniques are disclosed that locate implicitly defined semantic structures in a document, such as, for example, implicitly defined lists in an HTML document. The semantic structures can be used in the calculation of distance values between terms in the documents. The distance values may be used, for example, in the generation of ranking scores that indicate a relevance level of the document to a search query.

If a list on-page has a heading on it, the items on that list are considered equal distances away from the list. The words in the main heading on a page are considered equal distances away from that main heading. All of the words on a page are considered equal distances away from the title to that page. So, a page that is titled “Ford,” which has the word “motors” on that page, is considered to be relevant for the phrase “Ford Motors.” Here is an example of how that semantic closeness works with a heading and a list:

**Action Items**: Make Sure that titles, main headings, and headings for lists on pages are descriptive of the content they head and include keywords and related phrases that fit in with the pages’ content they appear upon.

**5. Page Speed**

Google has announced repeatedly that they consider Page Speed to be a ranking signal, including in the Google Blog post: [Using site speed in web search ranking](https://webmasters.googleblog.com/2010/04/using-site-speed-in-web-search-ranking.html), and also in a patent that I wrote about in the post, [Google’s Patent on Site Speed as a Ranking Signal](https://www.seobythesea.com/2014/02/googles-patent-site-speed-ranking-signal/).

The patent assigned to Google about Page Speed is [Using resource load times in ranking search results](http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO2&Sect2=HITOFF&p=1&u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsearch-adv.htm&r=1&f=G&l=50&d=PALL&S1=08645362&OS=PN/08645362&RS=PN/08645362). The patent tells us that this load time signal may be based upon measures of how long it takes a page to load on a range of devices:


> The load time of an online resource can be based on a statistical measure of a sample of load times for several different types of devices that might view the page or resource

**Action Items**: Review pages using tools such as [Page Speed for Insights](https://developers.google.com/speed/pagespeed/insights/) and [GTMetrix](https://gtmetrix.com/), to find recommendations on how to speed up the pages of a site. These will include many things such as reducing image file sizes, and redirects and broken links, extending browser cache times, minifying CSS and javascript, and more.

**6. Watch Times for a page**

While it may appear to be based upon videos, a Google Patent tells us that it may rank pages higher if they are watched for longer periods than other pages. The post I wrote about this patent on is: [Google Watch Times Algorithm For Rankings?](https://www.seobythesea.com/2015/08/google-watch-times-algorithm-for-rankings/#more-14919), and the patent it is about is, [Watch time based ranking](http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO1&Sect2=HITOFF&d=PALL&p=1&u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsrchnum.htm&r=1&f=G&l=50&s1=9,098,511.PN.&OS=PN/9,098,511&RS=PN/9,098,511).

A page may contain video or images or audio, and a watch time for those may make a difference.

**Action Items**: Keep an eye on the watch times reported upon for videos that you make to see if people stop watching them early, and identify where they might stop watching those. Then, make changes to make watching full videos something that people do.

**7. Context Terms on a Page**

I wrote the post [Google Patents Context Vectors to Improve Search](https://www.seobythesea.com/2016/10/google-patents-context-vectors-improve-search/), about the patent [User-context-based search engine](https://patents.google.com/patent/US9449105B1/en).

The patent tells us that it may look at words with more than one meaning in knowledge bases. For example, a bank, could mean a building money is stored in, or the ground on one side of a river, or what a plane does when it turns in the air. Then, the search engine may take terms from that knowledge base that shows what meaning was intended and collect them as “Context Terms.” It might look for those context terms when indexing pages to see what page those words are on to index the correct meaning.

**Action Items**: Visit knowledge base pages about the specific meanings of words, and find terms on those pages that might give your pages about the same meanings of those words more context. For example. If you are writing about Jaguars, and it could be the Jacksonville Jaguars, the breed of Cat, or the Apple Operating system, make sure to use words that help explain the context of the meaning of Jaguars that you have intended to write about. Getting that context right can help the search engine to know what you meant when optimizing your page for that term.

**8. Language Models Using Ngrams**

Google may give page quality scores based upon language models created from those pages when it looks at the ngrams on the pages of a site. This is similar to the [Google Book Ngram Viewer](https://books.google.com/ngrams).

I wrote about this in the post [Using Ngram Phrase Models to Generate Site Quality Scores](https://www.seobythesea.com/2017/09/site-quality-scores/) based upon the patent [Predicting site quality](http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO1&Sect2=HITOFF&d=PALL&p=1&u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsrchnum.htm&r=1&f=G&l=50&s1=9,767,157.PN.&OS=PN/9,767,157&RS=PN/9,767,157)

The closer the quality score for a page is to a high-quality page from a training set, the higher the page may rank.

**Action Items**: Creating high quality content should become a priority. That may require hiring experienced copywriters or proofreaders or editing programs such as Grammarly or Hemingway.

**9. Gibberish Content**

This may sound a little like #8 above. Google may use ngrams to tell if the words on a page are gibberish and reduce a page’s ranking. I wrote about this in a post titled, [Google Scoring Gibberish Content to Demote Pages in Rankings?](https://www.seobythesea.com/2013/10/google-gibberish-content-to-demote-pages/), about the patent [Identifying gibberish content in resources](https://patents.google.com/patent/US8554769B1/en).

Here is an ngram analysis using a well-known phrase, with 5 words in it:

The quick brown fox jumps
the quick brown fox jumps over
the brown fox jumps over the
fox jumps over the lazy
jumps over the lazy dog

Ngrams from a complete page might be collected like that, and from a collection of good pages and bad pages, to build language models (and Google has done that with many books, as we see from the Google Ngram Viewer covering a huge collection of books.) It would be possible to tell which pages are gibberish from such a set of language models. This Gibberish content patent also mentions a keyword stuffing score that it would try to identify.

**Action Items**: Google has been performing ngram analysis on lots and lots of books and documents. Of course, some programs will spit out gibberish that you can purchase or hire someone to produce content for you. Still, the chances are good that Google and visitors to your site will recognize low-quality content, which may cause Google to consider your site to be low quality and not rank it highly. On the other hand, if you have quality content, people may find value in it, return to your pages, and refer others to your site.

**10. Authoritative Results**

In the post [Authoritative Search Results in Google Searches?](https://www.seobythesea.com/2017/05/how-does-google-look-for-authoritative-search-results/), I wrote about the patent [Obtaining authoritative search results](http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO1&Sect2=HITOFF&d=PALL&p=1&u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsrchnum.htm&r=1&f=G&l=50&s1=9,659,064.PN.&OS=PN/9,659,064&RS=PN/9,659,064), which tells us that Google might look at the results of a search and if none of the Pages in the SERPs that appear are authoritative enough, it might search upon one of the query refinements that are listed with those results to see if they return any authoritative results.

If they do, they may merge the authoritative results into the original results. The way it describes authoritative results:


> In general, an authoritative site is a site that the search system has determined to include particularly trusted, accurate, or reliable content. The search system can distinguish authoritative sites from low-quality sites that include resources with shallow content or frequently include spam advertisements. Whether the search system considers a site to be authoritative will typically be query-dependent. For example, the search system can consider the Centers for Disease Control site, “cdc.gov,” to be an authoritative site for the query “CDC mosquito stop bites.” Still, it may not consider the same site to be authoritative for the query “restaurant recommendations.” A search result that identifies a resource on an authoritative site for the query may be authoritative.

**Action Items**: Google has been publishing [Quality Raters Guidelines](https://static.googleusercontent.com/media/guidelines.raterhub.com/en//searchqualityevaluatorguidelines.pdf) where they define what they mean by such terms as Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness. It can be beneficial to go through those guidelines and understand what they mean by those terms. They update those guidelines on an occasional basis, and it is worth keeping an eye out for when a new version comes out, so you can keep aware of any changes they may make to those.

**11. How Well Databases Answers Match Queries**

This patent doesn’t seem to have been implemented yet. But it might and is worth thinking about.

I wrote the post [How Google May Rank Websites Based Upon Their Databases Answering Queries](https://www.seobythesea.com/2017/03/how-google-may-rank-websites-databases-answering-queries/), based upon the patent [Resource identification from organic and structured content](http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO1&Sect2=HITOFF&d=PALL&p=1&u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsrchnum.htm&r=1&f=G&l=50&s1=9,589,028.PN.&OS=PN/9,589,028&RS=PN/9,589,028). It tells us that Google might look at searches on a site and how a site might answer them to see if they are similar to the queries Google receives from searchers.

If they are, it might rank results from those sites higher. The patent also shows us that it might include the database results from such sites within Google Search results. If you start seeing that happening, you will know that Google decided to implement this patent. Here is the screenshot from the patent:

**Action Items**: Google doesn’t appear to be using this patent yet, but they could. It ranks sites (potentially large sites such as job sites and travel sites) on the databases that supply answers to visitors of those sites. If the answers it returns are queries similar to the queries that users of a search engine perform, then the sites those databases are on will rank higher. You can get a sense of the questions that people may ask using Google’s keyword prediction tool. It is impossible to tell whether or not Google will implement this patent, but it is possible.

**12. Suspicious Activity to Increase Rankings**

Another time that Google publicly stated that “just because we have a patent doesn’t mean we use it came shortly after I wrote about a patent in a post I called [The Google Rank-Modifying Spammers Patent](https://www.seobythesea.com/2012/08/google-rank-modifying-spammers-patent/) based upon the patent [Ranking documents](http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO2&Sect2=HITOFF&p=1&u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsearch-adv.htm&r=1&f=G&l=50&d=PALL&S1=08244722&OS=PN/08244722&RS=PN/08244722).

It tells us about a transition rank that Google may assign to a site where they see activity that might be suspicious, such as keyword stuffing. Instead of improving the ranks of pages, they might decrease them or rerank them randomly. The motivation behind it appears to be to have those people making changes to do more drastic things. The patent tells us:


> Implementations consistent with the invention principles may rank documents based on a rank transition function. They may use the ranking based on the rank transition function to identify subjects subjected to rank-modifying spamming. The rank transition may provide confusing indications of the impact on rank in response to rank-modifying spamming activities. Implementations consistent with the invention principles may also observe spammers’ reactions to rank changes to identify manipulated documents actively.

**Action Items**: Learn about the [Google Webmaster Guidelines](https://support.google.com/webmasters/answer/35769?hl=en), and avoid doing things that Google might consider to be [manipulative](https://www.seobythesea.com/2007/11/google-patent-on-web-spam-doorway-pages-and-manipulative-articles/). If you make changes to your site to try to improve the SEO of the site, and you don’t see any impact from those changes, don’t panic, and don’t take any actions that Google might perceive to be negative.

**13. Popularity Scores for Events**

Might Google rank pages about events higher based upon how popular it might perceive that event to be? I wrote the post [Ranking Events in Google Search Results](https://www.seobythesea.com/2016/11/ranking-events-in-google-search-results/) about the patent [Ranking events](https://patents.google.com/patent/US9424360) which told us about popularity of an event being something that would make a difference. The following Screenshot from the patent shows some of the signals that go into determining a popularity score for an event:

Some patents provide a list of the “Advantages” of following a process in the patent, as does this one:

The patent describes the following advantages in following the approach it describes.

1. Events in a given location can be ranked so those popular or interesting events can be easily identified.
2. You can adjust the ranking to ensure that highly-ranked events are diverse and different from one another.
3. Events matching various event criteria can be ranked to easily identify those popular or interesting events can be easily identified.
4. You can provide the ranking to other systems or services that can use the ranking to enhance the user experience. For example, a search engine can use the ranking to identify the most popular events relevant to a received search query and present the most popular events to the user in response to the received query.
5. A recommendation engine can use the ranking to provide information identifying popular or interesting events to users that match the users’ interests.

**17. Site Quality Scores**

If you search for “seobythesea named entities,” it is a signal that you expect that you can find information about named entities on the site seobythesea.com.

If you do a site operator search such as “site:https://www.seobythesea.com named entities,” you again show that you expect to find information about a particular topic on this site. These are considered queries that refer to a particular site.

They are counted against queries that are considered to be associated with a particular site. So, if there are more referring queries than associated queries, the quality score for a site is higher.

If there are fewer referring queries than associated queries, then the quality score is lower. The post I wrote about this was [How Google May Calculate Site Quality Scores (from Navneet Panda)](https://www.seobythesea.com/2015/05/google-site-quality-scores/) based upon the patent [Site quality score](http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO1&Sect2=HITOFF&d=PALL&p=1&u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsrchnum.htm&r=1&f=G&l=50&s1=9,031,929.PN.&OS=PN/9,031,929&RS=PN/9,031,929). A lower site quality score can mean a lower rank, as the patent tells us:


> The site quality score for a site can be used as a signal to rank resources or to rank search results that identify resources that are found in one site relative to resources found in another site.

I wrote about this in the post [Ranking Local Businesses Based Upon Quality Measures including Travel Time](https://www.seobythesea.com/2017/02/ranking-local-businesses-based-upon-quality-measures-including-travel-time/#more-17830) based upon the patent [Determining the quality of locations based on travel time investment](http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO1&Sect2=HITOFF&d=PALL&p=1&u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsrchnum.htm&r=1&f=G&l=50&s1=9,558,210.PN.&OS=PN/9,558,210&RS=PN/9,558,210).

Would you drive an hour away for a slice of pizza? If so, it must be a pretty good pizza. The abstract from the patent tells us this:


> …the quality measure of a given location may be determined based on the time investment a user is willing to make to visit the given location. For example, the time investment for a given location may be based on comparing one or more actual distance values to reach the given location to one or more anticipated distance values to reach the given location.

**Action Items**: Sometimes Google Maps navigation doesn’t work correctly and will tell you in the middle of a highway that “You have arrived.” The place you were trying to get to isn’t in sight. Try it with your business and make sure that it works correctly to get people to your business. Showing up highly in search results in Maps listings is good, but not if Google loses people trying to get to your door. It’s worth checking upon if you haven’t. It does work most of the time correctly, but not all of the time. If it is having problems getting people to your door, make sure that the directions to your business on your site are straightforward to follow and that signage for your business is easy to see from the street.

**37. Reverse Engineering of Spam Detection in Local Results**

In the post [How Google May Respond to Reverse Engineering of Spam Detection](https://www.seobythesea.com/2016/07/fake-business-spam-detection/), I wrote about the patent [Reverse engineering circumvention of spam detection algorithms](http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO1&Sect2=HITOFF&d=PALL&p=1&u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsrchnum.htm&r=1&f=G&l=50&s1=9,372,896.PN.&OS=PN/9,372,896&RS=PN/9,372,896). I remembered how Google responded when people brought up the Google Rank-Modifying Spammers Patent that I wrote about in #13, telling people that just because they had a patent doesn’t mean they necessarily use it.

This patent is slightly different from the Rank modifying spammer’s patent. It only applies to local search, and it may keep a spamming site from appearing at all or appearing if continued activity keeps on setting off flags. As the patent abstract tells us:


> A spam score is assigned to a business listing when the listing is received at a search entity. A noise function is added to the spam score such that the spam score is varied. If the spam score is greater than the first threshold, the listing is identified as fraudulent, and the listing is not included in (or is removed from) the group of searchable business listings. If the spam score is greater than a second threshold, Google may flag the listing for an inspection less than the first threshold. The addition of the noise to the spam scores prevents potential spammers from reverse engineering the spam detecting algorithm. More listings submitted to the search entity may be identified as fraudulent and not included in the group of searchable listings.

**Action Items**: The Google support Page titled Improve your local ranking on Google introduces the things that Google looks at when ranking Local results in Google Maps. Google may look at rankings differently in [Was Google Maps a Proof? of Concept for Google’s Knowledge Base Efforts?](https://www.seobythesea.com/2014/09/google-maps-proof-of-concept/), which focuses upon structured data. When specific facts about a local entity are spammed, it throws off the consistency of those facts.

**38. Surprisingness in Business Names in Local Search**

Another patent that is about spam in local search is one I wrote about in the post [Google Fights Keyword Stuffed Business Names Using a Surprisingness Value](https://www.seobythesea.com/2015/09/google-fights-keyword-stuffed-business-names-using-a-surprisingness-value/) written about the patent [Systems and methods of detecting keyword-stuffed business titles](http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO1&Sect2=HITOFF&d=PALL&p=1&u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsrchnum.htm&r=1&f=G&l=50&s1=9,135,625.PN.&OS=PN/9,135,625&RS=PN/9,135,625).

This patent targets keyword-stuffed business names that include prominent business names to try to confuse the search engine. Examples include such names as “Locksmith restaurant” and “Courtyard 422 Y st Marriott.”

**Action Items**: The Google Local Guides program puts feet on the street to answer questions about places with unusual names, which is something that Google didn’t have and which can be helpful. If you haven’t looked into the [local guides program](https://maps.google.com/localguides/signup?utm_campaign=webapp&utm_source=web&utm_medium=o&utm_term=false&utm_content=), you should think about joining. Participating in it will give you an idea of the questions that they ask about businesses. It’s worth checking out.

**39. Local Expert Reviews**

I’ve heard people suggest that reviews can help a local search rank higher. I have seen reviews considered equivalent to a mention in the Google patent on Location Prominence. But, I’ve now also seen a Google patent which tells us that a review from a local expert might also increase the rankings of a local entity in local results. My post was At Google Local Expert Reviews May Boost Local Search Results on the patent [Identifying local experts for local search](http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO1&Sect2=HITOFF&d=PALL&p=1&u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsrchnum.htm&r=1&f=G&l=50&s1=9,792,330.PN.&OS=PN/9,792,330&RS=PN/9,792,330).

**Action Items**: People do perform searches to find reviews. If there aren’t reviews about a business type that I might be interested in, I will sometimes ask people whose opinions I value. Word of mouth can be valuable, and people often will look for opinions from people they perceive to be like them. One of the last reviews I left was at a tire store, which had a sign suggesting that people leave a review on their yelp page. The service was good, and they gave me a good deal. I left a review. This was interesting that Google might consider some reviewers to be local experts.

**40. Similar Local Entities**

When you search for a local coffeehouse, Google may decide to show you similar local businesses. It may include some other coffee houses or other similar results in what you see also. I wrote a post on this called [How Google May Determine Similar Local Entities](https://gofishdigital.com/google-similar-local-entities/), from the patent [Detection of related local entities](http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO1&Sect2=HITOFF&d=PALL&p=1&u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsrchnum.htm&r=1&f=G&l=50&s1=9,858,291.PN.&OS=PN/9,858,291&RS=PN/9,858,291).

**Action Items**: It’s good knowing that Google is willing to show similar businesses in searches that you might search for. Finding something that makes your business stand out in a way that could cause people to drive further to get to you is probably a good idea. I see that happen near me with many restaurants on Historic Highway 101 and are modeled after diners or historical places. Not necessarily an SEO approach, but what do you do to stand out?

**41. Distance from Mobile Location History**

Google keeps track of places that you may visit using a mobile device such as a phone. It may return results on searches based upon distance from you. It will look at the relevance of a business name to your search. It also looks at the [location prominence](https://www.seobythesea.com/2006/12/google-local-search-patent-application-on-ranking-businesses-at-a-location/) of a local entity to its location. The distance used to be from where you were searching, but it may now be based upon a distance from your location history. I wrote about this in [Google to Use Distance from Mobile Location History for Ranking in Local Search](https://gofishdigital.com/mobile-location-history/)

This is based upon a patent called [Ranking Nearby Destinations Based on Visit Likelihood and Predicting Future Visits to Places From Location History](https://patents.google.com/patent/US8949013)

**Action Items**: If your business is located near a busy corridor that many people travel down for their daily commutes, your business may show up as near them, when they search for different types of businesses if Google is showing them places based on a distance from a location history. I like having a directions page that shows how to visit local area landmarks – this doesn’t necessarily help your ranking. Still, it may help deliver visits to you from familiar with the area and know where the local highways and airports are.

**42. What People Search for at Locations Searched**

Search for a place that you might visit, and the query refinements that you might see may be based upon what people at that location you are considering visiting may have searched for when they were visiting that place. For example, the “Leo Carrillo” example above is for a ranch converted into a state park where many people get married, and chances are the queries shown are from people searching from that park.

This doesn’t affect the rankings of the results you see, but instead the query refinements you are shown. See [Local Query Suggestions Based Upon Where People Search](https://gofishdigital.com/local-query-suggestions-based-upon-where-people-search/) based on [Local query suggestions](http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO1&Sect2=HITOFF&d=PALL&p=1&u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsrchnum.htm&r=1&f=G&l=50&s1=9,626,454.PN.&OS=PN/9,626,454&RS=PN/9,626,454).

**Action Items**: If you are considering visiting a place you haven’t been to before, looking at the query refinements that appear at the bottoms of searches can give you ideas on what else people might search for when they are at that location. Knowing that may surface ideas for content you create on your site or marketing you might do in your area. It is a good thing to know about your location.

**43. Semantic Geotokens**

A semantic geotoken is a standardized representation for the geographic location, including one or more location-specific terms for the geographic location. My post about geotokens provides details on how much an impact they might have when shown in different ways, at [Better Organic Search Results at Google Involving Geographic Location Queries](https://gofishdigital.com/better-organic-search-results-at-google-involving-geographic-location-queries/)

These are based on a patent named [Semantic geotokens](http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO1&Sect2=HITOFF&d=PALL&p=1&u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsrchnum.htm&r=1&f=G&l=50&s1=9,582,548.PN.&OS=PN/9,582,548&RS=PN/9,582,548)

**Action Items**: There may be ways of including geographically relevant content on the pages of your site that can help it rank better for organic searches. Conversely, having your business rank well for map results and organic results can make it more likely that your business is seen in search results. Aim for both.


## Voice Search Google Ranking Signals

**44. Stressed Words in spoken queries**

This may not be something you can optimize a page for, but it does show that Google is paying attention to voice search and where that might take us. In the post [Google and Spoken Queries: Understanding Stressed Pronouns](https://www.seobythesea.com/2017/01/google-spoken-queries-understanding-stressed-pronouns/) based upon the patent [Resolving pronoun ambiguity in voice queries](http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO1&Sect2=HITOFF&d=PALL&p=1&u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsrchnum.htm&r=1&f=G&l=50&s1=9,529,793.PN.&OS=PN/9,529,793&RS=PN/9,529,793), we see that Google may be listening for our voices to emphasize certain words when we ask for something. Here is an example from the patent:

A voice query asks: “Who was Alexander Graham Bell’s father?”
The answer: “Alexander Melville Bell.”
A follow-up voice query: “What is HIS birthday?”
The answer to the follow-up query: “Alexander Melville Bell’s birthday is 3/1/1819”

**Action Items**: If you are interested in voice search at Google, you should read a case study they published in 2010: [Google Search by Voice: A case study](https://static.googleusercontent.com/media/research.google.com/en//pubs/archive/36340.pdf) It is about some of the technological issues that Google faced when setting up voice search. It also discusses some of the metrics that Google uses to determine the quality of its voice search. Language modeling and location impact on those language models makes a difference, too.


## News Search Google Ranking Signals

**45. Originality in News Search**

Google has a few patents that focus specifically on Google ranking signals for news results. They have updated some of those patents with continuation patents that have rewritten claims in them. I came across one that once focused upon geography as a critical signal but appears to pay much more attention to originality now. I wrote about that change in the post [Originality Replaces Geography as Ranking Signal in Google News](https://gofishdigital.com/originality-replaces-geography/)

The updated patent is [Methods and apparatus for ranking documents](http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO1&Sect2=HITOFF&d=PALL&p=1&u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsrchnum.htm&r=1&f=G&l=50&s1=9,477,714.PN.&OS=PN/9,477,714&RS=PN/9,477,714)

**Action Items**: The patent says “originality” but tells us that freshness and recency are also important. It looks like being the first to get a scoop on a topic and having the most original takes on that story still has a lot of value in news results. That probably wouldn’t come as a surprise to anyone covering the news. Making sure that it translates well to the written word is important.


## Additional Google Ranking Signals

**46. Global and Local Scores, Relevance, and Reliability**

Part of this approach is one that reminded me of Topic Sensitive PageRank. Some of it is based upon a global ranking for a site and onsite rankings for pages of a site. Different types of relevance may matter under this approach. This can include a Topical Relevance to other sites, Authoritative Relevance to other sites, and Off-site factors that indicate authority on site. Reliability is also a concern as well. Nevertheless, this does appear to be an alternative to PageRank that is worth considering as a ranking signal.

I wrote about this in a post titled [A Replacement for PageRank?](https://www.seobythesea.com/2014/12/replacement-pagerank/), and it is based upon the patent [Onsite and offsite search ranking results](https://patents.google.com/patent/US8843477).

**Action Items** Carefully consider the themes presented in the site and how much of the site covers each of those. Work to improve the relevance and authority, and reliability of all parts of a site.
